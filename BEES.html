Honey Bees

Honey bees are crucial, social insects that live in organized colonies to produce honey and pollinate plants.

They are vital pollinators, visiting countless flowers to collect nectar, which helps plants grow and supports ecosystems. Recognized by their buzzing sound and often yellow-and-black stripes, they work collaboratively and can fly up to 15 miles per hour.They live in colonies with a queen, workers, and drones, producing honey from nectar to store as food. Known for their complex communication via a "waggle dance," they are essential for ecosystem health.Bees have five eyes: two large compound eyes for spotting motion and three small simple eyes (ocelli) on top of their head to detect light.Bees do not sleep in the traditional sense, but they do rest, often holding onto flowers or staying quiet in the hive.Workers can create a new queen by feeding a larva a special diet called "royal jelly".Only female bees can sting; male bees (drones) have no stingers. Honey bees die after stinging because their barbed stinger remains in the victim.Bees have existed for approximately 30 million years. There are over 20,000 different bee species worldwide.Honey is the only food source that includes all substances necessary to sustain life, including enzymes, minerals, and water.Native to South America, vulture bees don't rely on flowers, instead foraging on carrion and creating a protein-rich honey. Bumblebees leave a scent in their footprints that helps them distinguish between their own scent, a relative's, and a stranger's, helping them find food. Bees have 170 odorant receptors, giving them a sense of smell 50 times more powerful than a dog.They cannot see the color red, but they can see ultraviolet light.When honey bees lose their barbed stinger, it causes a fatal injury, making it a last-resort defense.
